## Why This Matters Today

The consistent theme across today's stories is that AI agents are crossing from experimental to operational — and the infrastructure is growing up with them. OpenAI publishing empirical research on workplace transformation, Google shipping background task execution in its managed API, and Anthropic extending Claude Code to non-coders all point to the same shift: agents are being treated as production-grade software, not demos. At the same time, the economics are tightening. The Notre/Oxford debate over Claude Code pricing hitting $200 a month, the Linux Foundation building payment standards so agents can transact autonomously, and new benchmarks evaluating real-world task performance all signal that the industry is moving from "can we build agents?" to "can we afford to run them at scale?"

The most telling signal is product-level: Notion killing its email app because "most users use AI agents instead," and Salesforce rebuilding Slackbot from scratch as an autonomous agent. These are not experiments — they are bets that the agent-native future is arriving now. For teams and builders, the practical takeaway is that the window for evaluating agents is closing; the organizations winning right now are those treating agent capability, cost, and reliability as core infrastructure decisions rather than side projects.
