### 1. White House Pressures OpenAI to Slow-Roll GPT 5.6 Over Safety Concerns

**AI Policy** | June 25, 2026 | TechCrunch

OpenAI's newest model, GPT 5.6, won't see a public release. The Trump administration has told the company to limit distribution to select partners, with government "approving access customer by customer." The Office of the National Cyber Director and the Office of Science and Technology Policy reportedly worked closely with OpenAI on the rollout. CEO Sam Altman told staff that if the limited release goes well, a broader launch could follow a couple weeks later. The move signals a shift from the administration's earlier "hands-off" stance to active federal oversight of frontier model releases — mirroring what Anthropic is already doing voluntarily.

### 6. 2,000 People Tried to Hack His AI Assistant — Here's What Happened

**Security** | June 25, 2026 | Fernando Irarrázaval

Developer Fernando Irarrázaval built hackmyclaw.com as an open security experiment: anyone could email his AI assistant "Fiu" and try to extract a secrets.env file. After hitting the Hacker News front page, Fiu received over 6,000 emails from 2,000+ people. The secrets never leaked. Attacks ranged from authority impersonation to multi-language social engineering. Google suspended Fiu's Gmail due to fraud detection triggers, and the experiment cost $500+ in API calls — but proved that well-prompted assistants can withstand massive adversarial testing.

[Read full story](https://www.fernandoi.cl/posts/hackmyclaw/)

### 7. Overfitted a 900KB Transformer to Compress a 100MB CSV Into 7MB

**Research** | June 25, 2026 | Hacker News

A developer built an experiment using a tiny overfitted transformer (900KB) with arithmetic coding to compress files by memorizing them rather than learning general patterns. On a 100MB NYC taxi CSV, it achieved ~0.5 bits/byte (compressed to ~7MB). On enwik9 text, it hit ~1.68 bits/byte. The tradeoff: slow training (20-30 min) and compression/decompression (~45 min each) on an AMD 7800XT. The code is open-sourced on GitHub as pym-particles.

[Read full story](https://github.com/samyak112/pym-particles)
