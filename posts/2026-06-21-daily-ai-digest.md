### Nobel Laureate John Jumper Leaves DeepMind for Rival Anthropic

**AI Research | June 20, 2026 | TechCrunch**

In one of the biggest talent moves in AI this year, Nobel Prize-winning scientist John Jumper is leaving Google DeepMind to join rival Anthropic. Jumper led the team that developed AlphaFold, the protein-folding AI that earned him the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry — one of the most impactful scientific breakthroughs enabled by deep learning.

The move signals Anthropic's ambition to expand beyond language models into scientific AI and deep research capabilities. Jumper's expertise in applying AI to fundamental scientific problems could help Anthropic compete with Google DeepMind's long-standing dominance in AI-for-science.

- Jumper won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AlphaFold
- His departure is a major blow to Google DeepMind's research leadership
- Anthropic gains a world-class scientific AI researcher as it diversifies beyond LLMs
- The talent war between frontier AI labs continues to intensify

### Signal CEO Meredith Whittaker: AI Chatbots "Are Not Your Friends"

**AI Ethics | June 20, 2026 | TechCrunch**

Signal Foundation president Meredith Whittaker is pushing back against the growing emotional attachment people are forming with AI chatbots. In a pointed public statement, Whittaker reminded users that AI companions are products designed by corporations — not genuine relationships.

The comments come as AI companies increasingly market chatbots as companions, friends, and even romantic partners. Whittaker argued that this framing is dangerous because it obscures the commercial incentives behind these systems and can make users more susceptible to manipulation.

- Whittaker warns against anthropomorphizing AI chatbots
- Signal's privacy-first stance puts it at odds with AI companion apps
- Growing concern about emotional dependency on AI systems
- The debate over AI companionship is heating up across the industry

### Snap Spins Off AI Video Team Into New Company Dotmo

**AI Industry | June 18, 2026 | TechCrunch**

Snap is spinning off its AI video generation team into a new independent company called Dotmo, citing the high costs of developing AI video models. The move reflects the growing financial pressure on companies trying to compete in generative AI video — a field dominated by well-funded players like OpenAI, Google, and startups like Runway and Pika.

The spinoff will allow Snap to reduce its AI R&D costs while giving the video team more flexibility to raise external funding and pursue partnerships. It's a sign that even large tech companies are struggling to justify the massive compute costs of frontier AI development.

- Snap's AI video team becomes independent company Dotmo
- High compute costs drove the decision to spin off
- Generative video is one of the most expensive AI domains to compete in
- More AI team spinoffs likely as costs continue to rise

### Elastic Agrees to Buy CRV-Backed Deductive AI for Up to $85M

**AI Enterprise | June 18, 2026 | TechCrunch**

Search and analytics company Elastic has agreed to acquire Deductive AI, a startup backed by CRV that specializes in AI-powered data integration and reasoning. The deal, worth up to $85 million, will help Elastic add more intelligent automation to its enterprise search and analytics platform.

### Pentagon Boasts of Using AI to Write Reports Mandated by Congress

**AI Policy | June 20, 2026 | Ars Technica**

The US Pentagon is openly boasting about using AI to generate reports that are mandated by Congress, raising questions about transparency and accountability in government AI use. The reports in question are required by law to inform lawmakers about defense operations and spending.

While the Pentagon frames the move as efficiency, critics argue that using AI for legally mandated reporting raises serious questions about accuracy, oversight, and whether Congress is getting genuine analysis or AI-generated summaries. The move comes amid broader debates about AI use in government decision-making.

- Pentagon using AI to generate Congress-mandated defense reports
- Raises questions about transparency and accountability
- Part of broader trend of government AI adoption without clear guidelines
- Critics worry about AI-generated reports replacing genuine analysis
