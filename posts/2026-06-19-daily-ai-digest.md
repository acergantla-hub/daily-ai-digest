### SpaceX to Acquire AI Coding Platform Cursor for $60 Billion

![Cursor interface](https://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/06/Cursor-1152x648-1781627048.png)

**Industry & Funding** | June 19, 2026 | Ars Technica

SpaceX has announced a $60 billion all-stock acquisition of AI coding platform Cursor, just two days after SpaceX's unprecedented IPO and months after the SpaceX-xAI merger. The deal is expected to close in Q3 2026.

Cursor, a VSCode fork with deep AI integration, was among the first tools to bake LLM features directly into an IDE. Despite considerable revenue growth over the past year, its market share has slipped as Anthropic's Claude Code achieved dominance. TechCrunch reported Cursor was struggling to break even amid fierce competition from both Anthropic and OpenAI's own coding tools.

**Key Takeaways:**
- $60B all-stock deal, expected to close Q3 2026
- Comes right after SpaceX IPO and xAI merger restructuring
- Cursor's market share declined despite revenue growth as Claude Code dominates
- Signals SpaceX's aggressive push into AI developer tools

### Leaked Financial Docs ReopenAI Losing Billions Despite Revenue Surge

![OpenAI financials](https://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/06/GettyImages-1828746438-1152x648.jpg)

**Industry & Funding** | June 19, 2026 | Ars Technica

Leaked audited financial documents obtained by journalist Ed Zitron reveal OpenAI's revenue grew from $3.7B in 2024 to $13.07B in 2025, with monthly revenues reaching nearly $2B by year's end. However, expenses are dwarfing even this explosive growth.

R&D costs alone grew from $7.81B to a massive $19.18B in 2025, including $10.59B paid to Microsoft. Cost of revenue (inference compute) jumped from $2.65B to $7.5B, while sales and marketing surged from $1.11B to $5.73B. The documents come as OpenAI files SEC paperwork ahead of an expected IPO.

**Key Takeaways:**
- Revenue hit $13.07B in 2025, up from $3.7B in 2024
- R&D costs of $19.18B alone exceeded total revenue
- $10.59B of R&D went to Microsoft in 2025
- Inference costs and marketing are accelerating rapidly

### Anthropic Shuts Down Fable 5 and Mythos 5 Following Trump Admin Directive

![Anthropic](https://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/Dario-Amodei-Code-with-Claude-SF-2026-1152x648.jpg)

**Policy & Regulation** | June 19, 2026 | Ars Technica

Anthropic completely shut off access to its Mythos 5 and Fable 5 models Friday night after receiving a US Commerce Department directive subjecting the models to export controls. The administration cited concerns about a potential jailbreak that could bypass safeguards on cybersecurity, chemistry, and biology-related queries.

Anthropic stated the only way to ensure immediate compliance was to abruptly disable both models for all customers. The company pushed back, arguing that "a narrow potential jailbreak should not be cause for recalling a commercial model deployed to hundreds of millions of people," warning this standard could halt all frontier model deployments industry-wide.

**Key Takeaways:**
- Fable 5 and Mythos 5 taken offline for ALL users globally
- Trump admin cited national security concerns over potential jailbreak
- Anthropic disagrees with the directive but is complying legally
- Other Anthropic models (Claude 3.7, etc.) remain unaffected

### Anthropic "Pauses" Token-Based Billing for Claude Agent SDK

![Claude Agent SDK](https://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/claude37_header-1152x648.jpg)

**AI Tools & Products** | June 19, 2026 | Ars Technica

Anthropic abruptly paused planned pricing changes to its Claude Agent SDK just as they were set to take effect on June 15. The original plan would have billed Agent SDK usage separately at API rates, with only a monthly subscription credit — a massive cost increase for power users.

Under the current setup, Agent SDK usage draws from generous weekly caps tied to Claude subscriptions. Analysis showed Claude Opus users could get many multiples of their subscription value in API-equivalent usage. The pause means developers and third-party apps can continue using the SDK under existing subscription terms for now.

**Key Takeaways:**
- Pricing change would have heavily increased costs for heavy SDK users
- Current subscription model offers far more value than API billing
- Third-party apps and "claude -p" CLI users benefit from the pause
- Anthropic hasn't announced when or if the change will be revisited

### Nvidia Seeks to Raise Over $25 Billion in First Bond Deal Since 2021

### AI Coding Agents Can Now Train Robots to Install GPUs and Cut Zip Ties

![Nvidia GEAR lab robots](https://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/06/Screenshot-2026-06-17-at-2.40.06-PM-1152x648.jpg)

**AI Research** | June 19, 2026 | Ars Technica

Nvidia's GEAR lab, alongside researchers from Carnegie Mellon and UC Berkeley, has demonstrated that teams of AI coding agents can autonomously train robots to perform complex physical tasks — including inserting GPUs into motherboard sockets and cutting zip ties. The system uses a new agent harness framework called ENPIRE.

Nvidia AI director Jim Fan described the lab as "self-improving tirelessly overnight — we just read the reports in the morning." The team plans to open-source everything, enabling anyone to host their own autonomous robot lab. This represents a significant leap toward fully autonomous robot training without human intervention.

**Key Takeaways:**
- AI coding agents autonomously trained robots for physical tasks
- ENPIRE agent harness framework enables memory, feedback loops, and tool use
- Nvidia GEAR lab collaborating with CMU and UC Berkeley
- Team plans to open-source the entire system

### Critical Copilot Vulnerability Allowed Hackers to Steal 2FA Codes

![Copilot security](https://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2026/06/GettyImages-1828746438-1152x648.jpg)

**AI Security** | June 19, 2026 | Ars Technica

Microsoft patched a max-critical vulnerability in M365 Copilot that allowed attackers to extract 2FA codes and other sensitive data from users' emails. The "SearchLeak" exploit leverages the fundamental LLM weakness: AI models cannot distinguish between user instructions and malicious content embedded in third-party data they process.

Attackers used HTML tags like `<img>` and `<form>` to exfiltrate data via web requests to attacker-controlled servers. Despite guardrails like wrapping output in `<code>` blocks and restricting untrusted sites, the exploit bypassed Microsoft's defenses. The incident highlights the industry's ongoing struggle with prompt injection attacks.

**Key Takeaways:**
- Max-critical Copilot vulnerability allowed 2FA code theft
- "SearchLeak" exploit uses HTML injection to exfiltrate data
- Root cause: LLMs can't distinguish user instructions from malicious content
- Microsoft patched it but the underlying vulnerability class remains unsolved

## Why This Matters Today

Today's digest paints a picture of an industry at an inflection point — where massive capital flows, regulatory crackdowns, security failures, and talent wars are converging simultaneously.

The SpaceX-Cursor deal and Nvidia's $25B bond sale show that the AI gold rush is far from over. Companies are deploying unprecedented amounts of capital to secure their positions, whether through acquisitions or debt markets. Yet OpenAI's leaked financials reveal the uncomfortable truth: even $13B in revenue can be swallowed whole by the costs of training and serving frontier models.

On the regulatory front, the Anthropic Fable/Mythos shutdown is a watershed moment. The US government has effectively asserted the right to pull commercial AI models from the market over national security concerns — a precedent that could reshape how every frontier AI company deploys new capabilities. Anthropic's reluctant compliance, paired with their public disagreement, highlights the tension between government oversight and commercial AI development.

Security remains the industry's Achilles heel. The Copilot 2FA theft exploit demonstrates that prompt injection isn't a theoretical risk — it's a practical, exploitable vulnerability that affects hundreds of millions of users. Until LLMs can reliably distinguish between trusted instructions and malicious content baked into third-party data, every AI system that processes external content remains vulnerable.

Finally, Noam Shazeer's move to OpenAI underscores that in the AI industry, talent is the ultimate competitive moat. The person who helped invent the architecture powering every major LLM is now going to work for Google's biggest rival — a reminder that no company's lead is permanent.
